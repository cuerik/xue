Requirements for students:  

This is a different kind of course from those you have taken.  This course is designed to teach students the experimental approaches to solve scientific problems.  Students will learn how a scientific problem is raised and how the problem is approached.  This goal will be achieved by:

1.  The lectures will not simply be stating the facts.  The scientific thinking process will be introduced to the students.  The students are required to read some research papers/review articles/book chapters  before or after each lectures.  These articles will be made available to the students before each lecture.

2.  There will be two oral presentations about original research papers by each student during the semester.  The presentation will include: (a) a brief description of the background of the research, (b) questions the paper addresses, (c) key research experiments, (d) conclusion from the results and (e) discussion on the results.  Each presentation will be about 20 min.  Each paper will be presented by two or three students and discussed by another group of students.  The students who lead the discussion should read carefully the papers and ask presenters issues or points about the paper that you think are important or that you don’t quite understand.

Each article being presented by the students should be read carefully and critically by all students before the presentation/discussion sessions.  This requirement may be monitored at the beginning of the presentation sessions. Instead of having everyone to make copies of the papers for himself, the student presenting the paper is asked to make copies for everyone in the class one week before. 

3.  The students are required to actively participate in the discussions.  In the presentation and discussion sessions, the discussion will be focused on the papers presented.   Students are also encouraged to ask questions throughout the lectures.  You will learn from papers, my lectures,  lectures of your own and your classmates and discussions among  all of us.  

4.  The students are required to write three essays during semester.  These papers should state the problems the research articles address and how well the experiments address the problems.  The papers should discuss the research result and possibly propose approaches to study further questions.  We will have a few class sessions to discuss about it.  There will be a review session for each writing assignments.

5.  Reading related chapters from several cell biology or developmental biology text books should help.  "Molecular Biology of the Cell", 3rd edition, 1994.  Alberts et al., Garland is the best.  You may copy just related chapters.  For the best results, try to do relevant reading before the classes and after.  For some sessions, particularly those presentation/discussion sessions, advanced reading is required.

The required reading are original research articles and some review articles.  References and copies of the articles will be provided. 

6. Other important things

The final examination will be 2 hours open book exam.  Active participation of the class, particularly do all the reading is the key for you to do well.

Grades will be determined on the basis of the writing assignments (25%), the oral presentations (30%), the final examinations (20%), contribution to the classroom and paper discussions including participating the classes (25%). 

Because this is a small presentation/discussion class, being in the classroom itself is the major part of the study.  Missing multiple classes means loss of main activities and is thus not acceptable, because there is no way we can make up the discussion for you (unlike for the big lecture class, you can always get the material somewhere).  Students should make every effort to show up for every class, particularly when your classmates are presenting.
